Hype & Lack of Clarity

The term "Agentic AI" has recently surged in popularity, often invoked in discussions about the next wave of artificial intelligence. Yet, despite the hype, there is a striking lack of clarity about what exactly "agentic" means in this context. Is it a new paradigm, a rebranding of old ideas, or simply a buzzword? The ambiguity is compounded by marketing, media, and even academic discourse, which frequently use "agentic" to refer to a wide range of systems, from simple chatbots to complex autonomous organizations. This page aims to cut through the noise and provide a grounded, critical overview.

Traditional Agent-Based Systems Theory

Before "agentic AI" became a buzzword, the field of agent-based systems was well-established in computer science and artificial intelligence. In this tradition, an agent is typically defined as an entity that perceives its environment, reasons about it, and acts upon it to achieve goals [1,2]. Multi-agent systems (MAS) theory explores how collections of such agents interact, cooperate, or compete [3].

Key concepts include:

  • Autonomy: Agents operate without direct intervention.
  • Reactivity: Agents perceive and respond to their environment.
  • Proactivity: Agents take initiative to achieve goals.
  • Social ability: Agents interact with other agents or humans.

Classic references include Wooldridge & Jennings (1995) [1] and Russell & Norvig (2021) [2].

Current Deployed AI Agents

Today, "AI agents" are deployed in a wide variety of settings, from personal assistants (e.g., Siri, Alexa) to autonomous vehicles, trading bots, and large-scale multi-agent simulations. Some notable examples:

  • Personal Assistants: Apple Siri, Amazon Alexa, Google Assistant
  • Autonomous Vehicles: Waymo, Tesla Autopilot
  • Game Agents: AlphaGo, OpenAI Five
  • Robotic Process Automation (RPA): UiPath, Blue Prism
  • Trading Bots: Jane Street’s trading agents, crypto market makers (e.g., Wintermute, Uniswap v3 bots)
  • Multi-Agent Simulations: Smart grid management, traffic simulation (often used for real-world system management, but the agents operate within simulated environments)
  • Large Language Model (LLM) Agents: Auto-GPT, BabyAGI, Devin

These systems vary widely in their autonomy, learning capabilities, and real-world impact. Some are tightly engineered for specific tasks, while others (like LLM-based agents) are more general and open-ended.

Is Agentic AI Simply the Study of AI Agents?

Is "agentic AI" just a new name for the study of AI agents, or is there more to it? The answer is nuanced. While much of the current discussion around agentic AI draws on the language and concepts of agent-based systems, there is a growing sense that something new is happening:

Importantly, "Agentic AI" is often used to imply a shift toward increasing autonomy and goal-driven behavior across open-ended, real-world tasks—rather than simply executing scripted or pre-defined actions. In particular, "agentic" often implies a higher degree of goal-directedness, where systems are expected to pursue objectives with initiative and adaptivity.

  • Scale & Complexity: Modern agentic systems often operate at much larger scales and with more complex behaviors than traditional agents.
  • Learning & Adaptation: Many current agents leverage deep learning, reinforcement learning, and self-improving architectures.
  • Emergent Properties: Interactions between agents (and with humans) can lead to emergent, unpredictable behaviors.
  • Socio-technical Integration: Agentic AI is increasingly about integrating agents into broader social, economic, and technical systems.

For example, LLM "agents" typically wrap large language model API calls with planning, tool use, and reflection loops to mimic agency and pursue user-specified goals.

Thus, while agentic AI builds on agent-based theory, it also pushes into new territory, raising questions about autonomy, responsibility, and the boundaries between tool and collaborator.

Theory vs. Engineering: The Practical Gap

Traditional agent-based systems theory provides a valuable foundation, but the engineering and design of real-world agentic AI systems is often far more involved. Practical considerations include:

  • System integration: Connecting agents to sensors, APIs, and other software
  • Scalability: Handling large numbers of agents and real-time data
  • Robustness & Safety: Ensuring agents behave reliably in unpredictable environments
  • Human-in-the-loop: Designing for effective human-agent collaboration
  • Ethics & Governance: Addressing issues of control, transparency, and accountability

In practice, building agentic AI systems requires a blend of theory, engineering, and design thinking.

Relevant Technologies

Large Language Models (LLMs) Reinforcement Learning Multi-Agent Systems Robotic Process Automation (RPA) Automated Planning Robotics API Integration Agent Orchestration Frameworks

Large Language Models (LLMs)

LLMs like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are increasingly used as the "brains" of agentic systems, enabling flexible reasoning, planning, and communication [4,5].

Reinforcement Learning

RL provides a framework for agents to learn from interaction with their environment, optimizing behavior through trial and error [2,6].

Multi-Agent Systems

MAS theory underpins much of agentic AI, focusing on coordination, negotiation, and emergent behavior among multiple agents [3].

Robotic Process Automation (RPA)

RPA tools automate repetitive business processes, often acting as software agents that interact with digital systems [7].

Automated Planning

Planning algorithms enable agents to sequence actions to achieve goals, a core capability in both classical and modern agentic systems [2].

Robotics

Physical robots are embodied agents, requiring integration of perception, planning, and control [2,8].

API Integration

Modern agents often rely on APIs to access data, perform actions, and connect with other systems [9].

Agent Orchestration Frameworks

Frameworks like LangChain, CrewAI, AutoGen, and others help manage, coordinate, and scale agentic workflows [10,11].

Practical & Design Considerations

Beyond high-level concepts, building and deploying agentic AI systems requires mastery of both technical and design skills. Key areas to learn and consider include:

  • Programming Languages: Python, Go, JavaScript, C++
  • Frameworks & Libraries: Ray, LangChain, CrewAI, OpenAI Gym, ROS
  • Cloud & Infrastructure: Kubernetes, Docker, serverless platforms
  • Data Engineering: Pipelines, ETL, real-time data processing
  • Deployment: CI/CD, monitoring, scaling
  • Security: Authentication, authorization, data privacy
  • Design Decisions: Agent architecture, communication protocols, human-agent interface, fail-safes

The design and deployment of agentic AI is as much about engineering and systems thinking as it is about algorithms and theory.

What it means from a Designer's perspective:

Designing for agentic AI is about crafting the experience, interaction, and social framing around agentic systems—not just making them function, but making them usable, meaningful, and aligned with human contexts.

1️⃣ Interaction Design:

  • How will people understand what the agent is doing and why?
  • What signals, interfaces, and affordances communicate intent, state, and next actions?
  • How can users steer, trust, and correct the agent without confusion?

2️⃣ Human-Centered Framing:

  • Why does this agent exist? What human need does it serve?
  • What does "success" look like for the human, not just the agent?
  • How will people feel about the agent's autonomy and decisions?

3️⃣ Boundary and Trust Design:

  • Where do we want the agent to act automatically, and where should it ask?
  • How do we design handoffs between human and agent gracefully?
  • How do we surface uncertainty in a usable way?

4️⃣ Social & Ethical Context:

  • How does this agent reshape workflows, power dynamics, and accountability in its environment?
  • How do we ensure it respects norms, values, and user expectations?

Practical Example:

Engineers might ensure an LLM-based agent can autonomously summarise Slack threads and propose action items.

A designer will define:

  • ✅ How summaries are presented (bullet? narrative? with confidence scores?).
  • ✅ How the user can edit, accept, or reject proposals easily.
  • ✅ How the agent's voice fits the team culture.
  • ✅ How to avoid information overload or false confidence.

What does Designing for Agentic AI mean?

  • ✅ Crafting how autonomous systems fit into, extend, and respect human workflows, needs, and contexts.
  • ✅ Focusing on interaction, trust, clarity, and human-centered framing, not just functionality.

Key Design Questions:

  • 👁️ Visibility: How does the user understand what the agent is doing and why?
  • 🎛️ Control: Where should the agent act autonomously vs. ask?
  • 🤝 Trust: How do we design handoffs and uncertainty transparency?
  • 🫂 Fit: How does the agent align with workflows, team culture, and social norms?
